## Project Overview

This section covers the actual notebook analysis investigating whether **nighttime satellite light intensity** can be used as a proxy signal for **economic activity and GDP prediction** in South Africa.

At a high level, the project follows a well-established research idea: artificial light observed at night is often correlated with population concentration, electrification, infrastructure intensity, and production activity.

## Research Motivation

The modelling choices align with literature from remote sensing, development economics, and applied machine learning:

- **Nighttime lights as an economic proxy.** Henderson, Storeygard, and Weil (2012) showed that satellite-observed light can help measure economic growth, especially where official accounts are noisy or incomplete.
- **Regularized linear models.** Ridge regression and Lasso are appropriate baselines when the number of predictors is modest but collinearity is likely.
- **Tree ensembles and boosting.** Random forests and XGBoost are strong non-linear baselines when relationships may be thresholded or interaction-heavy.
- **Explainable boosting.** Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs) offer stronger predictive performance than simple linear models with better interpretability than black-box ensembles.
- **Dimensionality reduction.** PCA is a reasonable preprocessing step for the World Bank indicator matrix.

## Core Questions

1. Can provincial nighttime-light intensity alone explain variation in South Africa's GDP?
2. Do non-linear models outperform regularized linear baselines on the extracted light features?
3. Does adding broad macroeconomic indicators improve predictive accuracy beyond nighttime lights alone?
4. What are the trade-offs between accuracy and interpretability in this setting?

## Data Assets

The notebook accesses three main data groups:

- **Nighttime satellite rasters** stored under `Nightlight_Satellite_Images_GDP`
- **OpenStreetMap administrative boundaries** stored under `OSM`
- **World Bank indicators** stored under `WorldBank/SA_Indicators.csv`

## End-to-End Workflow

### 1. Environment setup

The notebook installs geospatial and cloud I/O dependencies including pyogrio, boto3, s3fs, rasterio[s3], earthpy, xgboost, interpret, and streamlit.

### 2. Cloud data access

The notebook authenticates to AWS, mounts an S3-backed file system with s3fs, and points to folders containing OSM boundaries, nighttime rasters, and World Bank indicators.

### 3. Boundary extraction

An OpenStreetMap extract for South Africa is downloaded locally, then administrative polygons are read from the multipolygons layer.

### 4. Raster preprocessing

Each nighttime raster is clipped to South Africa using `rasterio.mask.mask`. The clipped raster is thresholded with `np.where(raster > 7, raster, 0)` to suppress very low-intensity values.

### 5. Feature construction

For each trimmed raster year, the notebook sums nighttime-light values inside every province and builds a province-by-year feature table.

### 6. Model experiments

The project compares Ridge, Lasso, Random Forest, XGBoost, EBM, RF + PCA on World Bank indicators, and combined models using merged nighttime-lights + World Bank datasets.

## Key Takeaways

- **Nighttime lights are informative.** Even the simpler ridge and lasso baselines explain a substantial share of GDP variation.
- **Non-linear models help.** XGBoost and EBM outperform linear baselines, suggesting the mapping from light intensity to GDP is not purely linear.
- **Merged features dominate.** Combining nighttime lights with macro indicators produces very high R² values — though evaluation may be optimistic given the small annual sample.
- **Interpretability matters.** The EBM result is especially interesting because it offers strong performance without sacrificing inspectability.
