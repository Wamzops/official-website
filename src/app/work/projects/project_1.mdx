# Project Summary

## Introduction
>
> Traditional GDP estimates are authoritative but often come with reporting lags and coarse spatial granularity. Nighttime lights (NTL) observed from satellites have repeatedly been shown to correlate with human activity, electrification, urbanization, and economic output. This project proposes a GIS-based pipeline to estimate South Africa's GDP (national and subnational) by combining nighttime light intensity with administrative boundaries and complementary covariates.
>
> The core idea is to learn a mapping from features derived from NTL (and optional auxiliary data) to GDP targets (e.g., annual GDP, GDP by province), producing:


> - A reproducible geospatial ETL pipeline (ingestion → masking/cleaning → aggregation by admin unit)
> - Exploratory analysis showing the relationship between NTL and GDP over time
> - Baseline econometric and ML models for GDP inference (and optionally nowcasting/forecasting)
> - Maps and dashboards that communicate spatial patterns and model outputs

> ***Research Questions***
>
> 1. To what extent can nighttime lights explain variation in South Africa's GDP across time and across provinces?
> 2. Which NTL feature engineering choices (radiance summaries, thresholds, spatial smoothing, seasonality controls) are most predictive of GDP?
> 3. How sensitive are estimates to known NTL confounders (gas flares, ephemeral lights, sensor differences, load shedding, aurora, fires)?
> 4. What is the best strategy to produce robust subnational GDP estimates when ground-truth GDP is limited (temporal cross-validation, hierarchical models, transfer learning)?
> 5. How do auxiliary covariates (population, built-up area, road density, land use, electrification proxies) improve accuracy and interpretability?

## Elevator summary of the project
>
> - Use VIIRS/DMSP nighttime lights and GIS boundary aggregation to create province-level (and optionally district/municipality-level) features over time.
> - Train baseline models (linear/log-linear, ridge/lasso, random forest/XGBoost; optionally sequence models) to infer GDP.
> - Produce maps and time-series outputs to communicate economic activity patterns and uncertainty.
> - Deliver an extensible workflow that can be adapted to other countries once validated for South Africa.

# GDP Prediction Using Nighttime Satellite Imagery

## Project Overview

This project investigates whether **nighttime satellite light intensity** can be used as a proxy signal for **economic activity and GDP prediction** in South Africa. The notebook builds a full exploratory workflow: it connects to cloud-hosted geospatial data, extracts provincial nighttime-light intensity from raster imagery, merges those features with macroeconomic indicators, and compares several regression models.

At a high level, the project follows a well-established research idea: artificial light observed at night is often correlated with population concentration, electrification, infrastructure intensity, and production activity. Prior work has shown that nighttime lights can help estimate economic activity where conventional statistics are sparse or delayed, which makes this a sensible starting point for GDP modelling.

## Research Motivation and Rationale

The modelling choices in this notebook align with literature from remote sensing, development economics, and applied machine learning:

* **Nighttime lights as an economic proxy.** Henderson, Storeygard, and Weil (2012) showed that satellite-observed light can help measure economic growth, especially where official accounts are noisy or incomplete. Chen and Nordhaus (2011) similarly demonstrated that luminosity can proxy economic statistics across countries.
* **Remote sensing for development estimation.** Jean et al. (2016) showed that satellite imagery combined with machine learning can recover development-relevant signals such as poverty, reinforcing the idea that geospatial imagery can encode socioeconomic structure.
* **Regularized linear models.** Ridge regression and Lasso are appropriate baselines when the number of predictors is modest but collinearity is likely. Provincial nighttime-light series are highly correlated across space and time, so shrinkage is a reasonable first response.
* **Tree ensembles and boosting.** Random forests and XGBoost are strong non-linear baselines when relationships may be thresholded or interaction-heavy. They are useful when the effect of light intensity on GDP is not strictly linear.
* **Explainable boosting.** Explainable Boosting Machines (EBMs) are a good compromise when you want stronger predictive performance than a simple linear model but better interpretability than a black-box ensemble.
* **Dimensionality reduction.** Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a reasonable preprocessing step for the World Bank indicator matrix because the notebook uses hundreds of macro indicators over a relatively small number of annual observations.

## Core Questions

1. Can provincial nighttime-light intensity alone explain variation in South Africa's GDP?
2. Do non-linear models outperform regularized linear baselines on the extracted light features?
3. Does adding broad macroeconomic indicators improve predictive accuracy beyond nighttime lights alone?
4. What are the trade-offs between accuracy and interpretability in this setting?

## Data Assets Used in the Notebook

The notebook accesses three main data groups from an S3 bucket:

* **Nighttime satellite rasters** stored under Nightlight_Satellite_Images_GDP
* **OpenStreetMap administrative boundaries** stored under OSM
* **World Bank indicators** stored under WorldBank/SA_Indicators.csv

Within the workflow, the South Africa geometry is read from an OSM .pbf extract using pyogrio, then dissolved into a national mask for raster cropping. Provincial boundaries are also retained so the notebook can aggregate nighttime-light values by province. A World Bank indicator table is loaded for the years 1992-2022 and used both for GDP labels and for broader macroeconomic feature engineering.

## End-to-End Workflow

### 1. Environment setup

The notebook installs geospatial and cloud I/O dependencies including pyogrio, boto3, s3fs, rasterio[s3], earthpy, xgboost, interpret, and streamlit.

### 2. Cloud data access

The notebook authenticates to AWS, mounts an S3-backed file system with s3fs, and points to folders containing OSM boundaries, nighttime rasters, and World Bank indicators.

### 3. Boundary extraction

An OpenStreetMap extract for South Africa is downloaded locally, then administrative polygons are read from the multipolygons layer with admin_level = '4'. Those boundaries are used both as a country mask and as province-level aggregation units.

### 4. Raster preprocessing

Each nighttime raster is clipped to South Africa using rasterio.mask.mask. The clipped raster is thresholded with np.where(raster > 7, raster, 0) to suppress very low-intensity values, then written back as a trimmed GeoTIFF.

### 5. Feature construction

For each trimmed raster year, the notebook sums nighttime-light values inside every province and builds a province-by-year feature table. That table becomes the main design matrix for the nighttime-lights-only models.

### 6. GDP target alignment

The notebook loads World Bank indicators from SA_Indicators.csv, transposes the data into a year-indexed table, and uses GDP (current US$) as the response variable.

### 7. Model experiments

The project compares:

* Ridge regression
* Lasso regression
* Random forest regression
* XGBoost regression
* Explainable Boosting Machine regression
* Random forest on PCA-compressed World Bank indicators
* Ridge, random forest, and voting regressor on the merged nighttime-lights + World Bank dataset

### 8. Lightweight deployment stub

The notebook ends by writing a minimal streamlit.py file, suggesting an eventual path toward interactive presentation or deployment.

## Why These Steps Make Sense

### Nighttime-light extraction by province

Aggregating raster intensity over provinces is a defensible way to convert imagery into tabular features for GDP modelling. This reduces very large raster data into economically interpretable administrative units, which matches how regional GDP is commonly reported.

### Thresholding low-intensity values

The line np.where(raster > 7, raster, 0) appears to be a denoising step. That is sensible because low digital-number values in nighttime-light products can capture background noise, dim ephemeral signals, or non-economic illumination. The exact threshold should ideally be validated, but the intuition is consistent with remote-sensing preprocessing practice.

### Linear and non-linear model comparison

Using both regularized linear models and tree/boosting models is a strong experimental design choice. Linear models test whether the signal is mostly additive and smooth; ensemble methods test whether the relationship contains interactions, saturation effects, or province-specific non-linearities.

### PCA for macro indicators

The World Bank matrix has 342 indicators with roughly 31 yearly observations after transposition. PCA is therefore a pragmatic way to compress redundant macro indicators before fitting a model and helps reduce instability from a high-feature, low-sample regime.

## Interpretation of Results

Several patterns stand out:

* **Nighttime lights are informative.** Even the simpler ridge and lasso baselines explain a substantial share of GDP variation.
* **Non-linear models help.** XGBoost and EBM outperform the linear baselines on the nighttime-light-only feature set, suggesting that the mapping from regional light intensity to GDP is not purely linear.
* **Merged features dominate.** Combining nighttime lights with macro indicators produces extremely high R^2 values, which probably means the merged dataset contains strong predictive information, but also that the evaluation may be too optimistic for such a small annual sample.
* **Interpretability matters.** The EBM result is especially interesting because it offers strong performance without giving up inspectability to the same degree as XGBoost.
