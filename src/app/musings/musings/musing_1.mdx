## Introduction

I had never really thought about or acknowledged my privilege of access to food until I was selected as one of the seven volunteers from IEEE Kenyatta University to join the Uvumbuzi Tech Challenge. The challenge was part of InZone, a research and project centre operating under the auspices of the University of Geneva. The organisation delivers higher-education resources to low-resource settings, particularly refugee communities. At the time, its programs were active in Kakuma Refugee Camp in Kenya and Azraq Refugee Camp in Jordan. I joined the Kakuma and Kalobeyei team as a trainer, where I contributed to the design and implementation of an engineering curriculum that was centered on the learners’ lived experiences and tailored for creative and entrepreneurial youth and overage learners at the camp. Further, both the trainees and trainers were to explore how community-identified challenges could be addressed through human-centred, technology-driven solutions.

In this blog, **I will share my experience of participating in the Uvumbuzi Tech Challenge, including the outcomes of my team’s efforts and the valuable engineering lessons I learned.** Additionally, I aim to provide insight into the realities of life in the largest refugee camp in Kenya and how we aim to apply human-centered design principles to make a significant positive impact.


## The Opportunity

In May 2021, the program, which would become the foundation of my passion for engineering for social good was launched. As background, Kakuma is the largest refugee camp in Kenya with a population of about over 309,444 with 225,739 people in Kakuma Camp and 83705 living in the Kalobeyei settlement as of October 2025. At the time, only about 5% of the population had access to electricity, while 69% of the population could access the internet using smartphones. This meant that there was a valid window, though narrow, for these creative people to share their experiences with us remotely and collaborate in co designing technology solutions for their most pressing problems.

### Infrastructure and Resource Constraints

* **Electricity Access:** Only about **5%** of the population has access to the formal power grid or consistent electricity. Most residents rely on small-scale solar home systems or expensive community-managed diesel generators.
* **Water Access:** Water scarcity is a critical constraint. While the **Kakuma Kalobeyei Challenge Fund (KKCF)** supports over **600 farmers** (60% of whom are women), irrigation remains a primary challenge for food security.
* **Food Security:** Subsistence farming is a vital livelihood, but displacement has often stripped skilled farmers of the land and tools they need to be self-sufficient.
* **Internet Penetration:** Approximately **69%** of the population has access to the internet via smartphones.
* **Connectivity Medium:** Mobile data (3G/4G) is the primary way residents connect with the outside world, using platforms like WhatsApp for community organization and even agricultural training.
* **Tech Ecosystem:** The presence of the **InZone Fabrication Lab** and various innovation hubs has turned the camp into a testing ground for human-centered design (HCD) and engineering for social good.


## The Uvumbuzi Tech Challenge

Why name the challenge Uvumbuzi Tech Challenge? Well, “Uvumbuzi” is a Swahili word that translates to “innovation”. Tech is a shorthand for technology and challenge refers to the act of justifying something. Putting the three together and you have our core business: Innovating using technology and proving the utility of our solutions in the camp and settlement context. Students from Kenyatta University (Kenya), ECE Graduate School of Engineering (France), IEEE (Kenya branch), and the Geneva Tsinghua Initiative (Switzerland/China) joined hands with those from Kakuma and Kalobeyei in the challenge to converge thoughts and provide an output in form of a solution prototype.

## Overview of the challenge

The challenge consisted of six project tracks: Power/Electricity, Waste Management, Cooking, Water Management, Social Issues, and Agricultural Technologies. I chose to be part of the team that focused on designing a tech solution to solve crop irrigation for farmers in the community. This task was centered on how to improve, secure and diversify agricultural production and gardening through sustainable and resilient local food production. ## The messy in-between

In collaboration with two team members who were on site, we conducted WhatsApp interviews with 30 farmers engaged in subsistence agriculture at the camp to better understand their challenges and inform our problem definition. These conversations revealed that many refugees and asylum seekers originated from rural areas and possessed valuable agricultural skills as crop producers, livestock keepers, or fishermen. However, displacement had stripped them of their livelihoods and economic stability. Notably, a key challenge that emerged was limited access to water, even for 16 of the interviewees who were growing vegetables in greenhouses provided by the Kakuma Kalobeyei Challenge Fund (KKCF), which trains over 600 farmers (60% women) in water-efficient farming.

This led us to frame three main questions to guide our solution:&#x20;

1. How can we ensure a consistent water supply to vegetable crops even during prolonged dry seasons?&#x20;
2. How can small-scale agriculture be made climate-resilient in low-resource settings?&#x20;
3. How can low-cost, locally maintainable irrigation systems improve productivity and sustainability?

## Our solution

To address these challenges, we collaborated with 15 learners to design 3D digital models using Onshape, a free 3D modelling software with minimal setup, for modular 3D-printed components used in soil-based vertical farming within the greenhouses, as well as 3D-printed pumps to deliver water through drip systems to the towers. This solution was chosen, as vertical towers would ensure more efficient use of the water available and greenhouse space, resulting in increased agricultural produce. Further, the 3D-printed pump, customized for drip lines, could optimize water delivery for each plant, and the water could be recaptured and recirculated, making the system more sustainable.



## Conclusion

After three months of collaboration and training, we produced a working prototype at the fabrication lab. While this did not materialize into a full-scale design, it provided critical insights into the feasibility of our concept and highlighted our practical limitations. It also helped the learners gain experience in applying theoretical knowledge to real-world challenges, as well as the importance of integrating community needs into technical solutions. This project transformed my understanding of food security from a distant concept into an all-consuming responsibility which I have since felt compelled to help sustain. Interacting directly with the learners also taught me the subtleties of integrating multiple perspectives into a shared vision, which in many ways prepared me for performing innovative, socially impactful engineering work.
